## Section 1 — What the article is about (~1.5 minutes)

### Key concept (simple definition)
**Academic collocations** = word combinations that commonly “go together” in academic speech, like:
- “raise a question”
- “reach a conclusion”
- “in this study”

### The problem/gap
Students can know vocabulary and grammar but still sound unnatural because collocations are hard to learn from rules. Meanwhile, AI tools are widely used, but teachers still don’t know the best way to design AI learning tasks:
- **Guided AI tasks:** structured prompts/steps
- **Unguided AI tasks:** open exploration with AI

### Research questions
1) Does **guided** use of ChatGPT lead to better learning than **unguided** use?  
2) Do **AI-supported tasks** lead to better learning than **non-AI** instruction?

**Presenter notes:**
- **Content/Structure:** Define collocations first, then introduce “guided vs. unguided.”
- **Delivery:** Use contrastive stress: “guided” vs. “unguided.”
- **Visual Aids:** Two-lane graphic: “guided path” vs. “free exploration.”
- **Language:** “The impetus for this study stems from…”, “The pressing question is…”

## Section 2 — What the researchers did (~1 minute)

### Study design (no heavy jargon)
- Upper-intermediate university learners (B2)
- **Three groups:**
  - **Unguided AI:** open interaction with ChatGPT during task preparation
  - **Guided AI:** structured activities + prompts with ChatGPT
  - **Control:** technology-mediated learning without AI
- Timeline: pretest → course tasks → posttest → delayed posttest

### What they measured (plain language)
They checked how often students used academic collocations, and how accurately they used them.

**Presenter notes:**
- **Content/Structure:** Keep methods short; focus on “fair comparison” (3 groups + multiple time points).
- **Delivery:** Point to the timeline; slow down on “three groups.”
- **Visual Aids:** Simple 3-column group diagram + timeline.
- **Language:** “We employed a comparative design…”, “To ensure robustness…”

## Section 3 — Key findings (~2 minutes)

### Finding 1 (main surprise): Unguided AI did best overall
Students who interacted with ChatGPT more freely tended to use academic collocations **more frequently and more accurately** than the guided group.

### Finding 2: Both AI groups beat the non-AI control
Regardless of guidance level, students using ChatGPT produced richer collocational output than the control group.

### Finding 3: Improvement exists, but learning is slow
Over the study period, gains were present but **modest**, suggesting collocations need repeated exposure and practice—AI helps, but it doesn’t make learning instant.

**Presenter notes:**
- **Content/Structure:** Present results in “surprise → pattern → realistic takeaway” order.
- **Delivery:** Use a brief pause before “Unguided did best” to create interest.
- **Visual Aids:** One simple chart: Unguided > Guided > Control; plus a small timeline or the paper’s “over time” figure if you use it.
- **Language:** “The findings reveal…”, “Our data indicates…”, “The analysis demonstrates…”

## Section 4 — Why it matters (~1.5 minutes)

### Significance for teaching and learning
This study challenges a common assumption: **more structure isn’t always better**. For learners who are already reasonably proficient, open exploration with AI may encourage more noticing and experimentation with useful phrases.

### Significance for AI task design
The key message is not just “AI helps,” but **how you design the task shapes what learners do with AI** (engagement, experimentation, uptake).

### Broader takeaway (non-specialist framing)
As AI becomes normal in education, we need evidence-based decisions about when to provide strict guidance and when to allow autonomy.

**Presenter notes:**
- **Content/Structure:** Translate “guidance vs. autonomy” into everyday teaching choices.
- **Delivery:** Emphasize the takeaway sentence slowly.
- **Visual Aids:** A slider graphic: “More guidance” ←→ “More autonomy,” with “depends on learners/tasks.”
- **Language:** “The implications of these findings suggest that…”, “This is particularly important because…”

## Section 5 — Impact on my own research + writing skills (~1.5 minutes)

### Impact on my research design (transferable lessons)
- Match the amount of guidance to learner level and task familiarity.
- Use a clear comparison design (AI vs. non-AI; guided vs. unguided) rather than assuming AI is automatically beneficial.
- Plan for outcome measures that reflect the skill you care about (frequency + accuracy of target language features).

### Good writing skills I learned (with evidence from the article’s structure)
- **Clear research-story logic:** problem → gap → questions → design → results → implications.
- **Strong signposting:** research questions are clearly stated and then revisited in the discussion.
- **Transparent limitations:** the paper openly notes constraints (e.g., what was measured and what should be tested next), which increases credibility.

**Presenter notes:**
- **Content/Structure:** Make this section personal (“If I do a similar project, I would…”).
- **Delivery:** Slow down and use a slightly warmer tone—this is the reflective part.
- **Visual Aids:** Two-column slide: “Research design” vs. “Writing moves.”
- **Language:** “This article has influenced how I think about…”, “From a writing perspective…”
