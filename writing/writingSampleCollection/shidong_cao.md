# Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success across a wide range of natural language understanding and generation tasks [Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2025]. A key factor behind this progress is the effectiveness of pre-training objectives based on corrupted-input reconstruction, such as masked language modeling and denoising sequence-to-sequence learning [Devlin et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020]. These objectives train models to recover clean text from partially corrupted inputs, thereby encouraging the acquisition of contextual and semantic representations. In particular, BERT-style masking has shown that reconstructing missing content from surrounding context is a powerful paradigm for language representation learning.

However, most existing corruption strategies primarily perturb *token identity* rather than *sequence structure*. Masking, deletion, or infilling removes lexical information, but often leaves the original word order either unchanged or only weakly constrained. As a result, models may become highly effective at predicting which words should appear, while being less explicitly trained to understand how those words should be organized into a coherent sequence. This limitation is important because natural language meaning depends not only on lexical content, but also crucially on word order and structural composition. Even when all words are preserved, a small local reordering can disrupt syntax, alter semantic scope, and degrade overall sentence coherence.

This gap suggests the need for a complementary denoising paradigm that focuses on *structural corruption*. Rather than hiding tokens, one can preserve lexical content and instead perturb the relations among tokens, forcing the model to recover the underlying order. Such a formulation is particularly appealing because it directly targets the compositional nature of language. Compared with semantic denoising, structural denoising asks the model to reason about dependency, locality, and global sequence consistency. In this sense, it can be viewed as a natural extension of BERT-style corrupted-text modeling from “what token is missing” to “where should each observed token be placed.”

A straightforward way to introduce structural corruption is to shuffle words. Nevertheless, fully random shuffling is often too destructive: it breaks nearly all local continuity, produces highly unnatural inputs, and may weaken the connection to realistic structural errors encountered in text editing, generation, or transmission. By contrast, *adjacent-word swaps* and other forms of local shuffling provide a more controlled and linguistically plausible corruption process. Such noise preserves most lexical evidence while progressively disturbing word order, yielding a continuum from mild local disorder to severe structural corruption. This makes local permutation noise a particularly suitable testbed for studying whether language models can learn robust order-sensitive representations.

Once the corruption is defined in this way, the restoration problem becomes fundamentally different from standard language generation. The target output is typically not a new sentence with arbitrary lexical choices, but a reordering of the tokens already present in the corrupted input. Standard autoregressive decoders are unnecessarily flexible for this setting: because they generate from the full vocabulary, they may duplicate, omit, or hallucinate tokens, even though the desired solution is essentially a permutation of the input sequence. This mismatch motivates a more structure-aware decoding mechanism. Pointer networks provide a natural solution, as they directly model output positions by selecting elements from the input, making them especially well suited for sequence restoration tasks defined over permutations.

Motivated by these observations, we propose a structure-aware text denoising framework that introduces *structural noise* through adjacent-word swaps (or more general local shuffling operations) and learns to reverse this process with a pointer-based restoration module. The forward process progressively corrupts sentence order while preserving lexical content, and the reverse process predicts how the scrambled tokens should be rearranged into a coherent sequence. In this way, our framework explicitly trains language models to capture structural regularities beyond token identity, encouraging sensitivity to local ordering cues and global sentence-level consistency.

Conceptually, our framework is inspired by the success of BERT-style denoising, but differs from it in one crucial aspect: instead of reconstructing missing words, we reconstruct *missing order*. This shift is important because order recovery isolates a dimension of language understanding that is often only implicitly learned in conventional objectives. By combining structural corruption with pointer-based restoration, our method provides a direct and principled way to study sequence organization, offering a complementary pre-training or fine-tuning signal for models that need stronger structural reasoning ability.

## Contributions

- We propose a structure-aware denoising framework for text that injects controllable structural noise through adjacent-word swaps or local shuffling, explicitly targeting order-sensitive language understanding.  
- We formulate the restoration of corrupted text as a permutation prediction problem and introduce a pointer-based decoding mechanism that preserves lexical fidelity while recovering coherent word order.  
- We provide a new perspective on corrupted-input learning by extending BERT-style denoising from token reconstruction to structural reconstruction, highlighting the importance of sequence organization in language modeling.  
- We demonstrate that learning to undo structural corruption improves the model's ability to capture compositional dependencies and recover coherent sequences under word-order perturbations.

## Related Work

### Diffusion Language Models

Diffusion models were first established as a powerful generative framework in continuous domains and later extended to discrete state spaces, making them applicable to text modeling. Early work such as D3PM demonstrated that diffusion processes can be defined over discrete tokens, bridging diffusion modeling with masked and autoregressive perspectives \cite{d3pm}. Building on this idea, Diffusion-LM explored diffusion in continuous text representations for controllable generation \cite{diffusionlm}, while DiffuSeq extended diffusion models to conditional sequence-to-sequence generation \cite{diffuseq}. DiffusionBERT further highlighted the compatibility between diffusion objectives and BERT-style denoising pre-training, showing that diffusion can be naturally integrated with masked language modeling \cite{diffusionbert}. 

More recent work, including SSD-LM/MDLM, LLaDA, and adaptation-based approaches such as DiffuLLaMA, has pushed diffusion language modeling toward more scalable and competitive large-language-model regimes \cite{mdlm,llada,diffullama}. These methods improve sampling efficiency, training stability, and scalability, bringing diffusion-based language models closer to the performance of large autoregressive models.

Despite this rapid progress, most existing diffusion language models are designed for general text generation, conditional generation, or scaling efficiency. They primarily focus on recovering semantic content under masking or noise, rather than explicitly modeling word-order corruption and structural restoration. Consequently, the potential of diffusion for text order recovery remains underexplored.

### Diffusion for Structural Restoration and Permutation Modeling

Beyond standard text generation, diffusion has increasingly been explored for discrete and structured objects, including graphs and other permutation-sensitive domains. This line of work suggests that diffusion is not limited to continuous signals, but can also serve as a general framework for recovering structured configurations from corrupted states.

In parallel, prior NLP research on generation order, such as insertion-based decoding and arbitrary-order generation, has shown that text generation need not follow a fixed left-to-right process \cite{insertiontransformer,levenshteintransformer}. These studies underline the importance of explicitly modeling order as a first-class object rather than treating it as an implicit byproduct of autoregressive decoding.

However, sequence restoration under local structural corruption remains insufficiently studied. In our setting, the corrupted input already contains the target lexical items, and the main challenge is to reconstruct their correct order. This makes the problem fundamentally different from unconstrained language generation.

Pointer Networks \cite{vinyals2015pointer} are especially relevant in this context because they are designed for outputs that correspond to selections or permutations of the input elements. Yet, to our knowledge, existing work has rarely combined diffusion-style structural corruption with pointer-based restoration for text reordering. This motivates our framework, which connects diffusion-based denoising with permutation-aware decoding for word-order recovery.
